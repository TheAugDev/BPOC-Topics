Racial Profiling & Investigative Stops

Basic Peace Officer Course | Chapter 6 Interactive Study Guide

Key Concepts & Definitions

What is Racial Profiling?

(Click or press Enter to reveal)

A law enforcement-initiated action based on an individual's race, ethnicity, or national origin rather than on the individual's behavior or on information identifying them as having engaged in criminal activity. (CCP 28.0051)

What is Reasonable Suspicion?

(Click or press Enter to reveal)

Sufficient articulable facts & circumstances, and reasonable inferences from those facts, which would lead a reasonable officer to conclude that criminal activity is afoot. The basis for temporary detentions (Terry Stops).

What is Probable Cause?

(Click or press Enter to reveal)

A reasonable person standard to believe that a crime has been, is being, or will be committed. It is a higher standard than reasonable suspicion and is required for arrests and searches.

The concept originated with the DEA's "Drug Courier Profile" in the 1970s, initially for use at airports. It was based on behavioral characteristics, not race.

  • Unusual nervousness
  • Ticket paid in cash
  • Travel to/from a known drug source destination
  • Carrying little or no luggage
  • Excessive travel, immediate phone use on landing

This was later adapted (and sometimes misapplied) to vehicle stops.

  • It's a direct conflict with American democratic principles.
  • It is dehumanizing and erodes public trust, harming law enforcement's effectiveness.
  • It's self-fulfilling bad logic: focusing on one group leads to disproportionate enforcement, reinforcing false beliefs.
  • It wastes resources by overlooking criminals of all races.
  • It generates suspicion and antagonism, making future legitimate stops more volatile.
  • Stop is based on a minor or contrived pretext traffic violation.
  • Driver/passengers are questioned about matters unrelated to the violation.
  • Occupants are ordered out of the vehicle.
  • Officers visually check all observable parts of the vehicle.
  • Consent to search is requested; if refused, intimidation or delay tactics may be used (e.g., waiting for K-9).

Texas Legal Framework

Each agency MUST adopt a detailed written policy that:

  • Clearly defines and strictly prohibits racial profiling (CCP 28.0052).
  • Implements a complaint process and provides public education on it.
  • Requires corrective action for officers found in violation.
  • Requires data collection for all motor vehicle stops (citations, warnings, arrests).
  • Requires chief administrator to submit an annual report to TCOLE and the governing body.

For each motor vehicle stop, an officer MUST report:

  • Physical description of the person detained (gender, race/ethnicity).
  • The initial reason for the stop.
  • Whether a search was conducted and the basis for it (e.g., consent, PC).
  • Description of any contraband found.
  • Whether an arrest was made and the basis/offense.
  • Location of the stop.
  • Whether a warning or citation was issued.
  • Whether physical force resulting in bodily injury was used.
  • Offense if a person:
    • Intentionally refuses to give name, residence, or DOB to a peace officer who has lawfully arrested them. (Class C Misdemeanor)
    • Intentionally gives a false or fictitious name, residence, or DOB when lawfully arrested, lawfully detained, or is a witness. (Class B Misdemeanor)
  • A person cannot be required to ID without a chargeable offense. The refusal to ID only becomes an offense *after* an arrest or during a traffic stop.
  • CCP 28.0102: Agencies may apply to the governor's office for grants to defray the cost of body-worn cameras. Agencies receiving a grant must match 25% of the funds.
  • CCP 28.0103: Agencies with body cameras may apply for grants to help with the cost of data storage.

Occupations Code 1701.253 & 1701.402

Requires TCOLE to establish a statewide comprehensive education and training program on racial profiling. An officer must complete this training as a requirement for an intermediate proficiency certificate.

Key Court Cases

Whren v. United States (1996)

Pretext Stops

Holding: Any traffic offense is a legitimate basis for a stop, regardless of an officer's subjective motivations. Pretext stops are constitutional.

Terry v. Ohio (1968)

Stop and Frisk

Holding: Police may stop a person on reasonable suspicion of criminal activity, and may frisk for weapons if there is reasonable suspicion the person is armed and dangerous.

Pennsylvania v. Mimms (1977)

Ordering Driver Out

Holding: Police can order the driver of a lawfully stopped car out of the vehicle for officer safety.

Maryland v. Wilson (1997)

Ordering Passengers Out

Holding: Extends the *Mimms* rule to passengers. Police may order passengers out of a lawfully stopped vehicle.

Arizona v. Johnson (2009)

Frisking Passengers

Holding: Police may frisk a passenger during a lawful traffic stop if they have reasonable suspicion that the person is armed and dangerous.

Rodriguez v. United States (2015)

Duration of Stop

Holding: A police stop cannot be prolonged beyond the time needed to handle the matter for which the stop was made, unless new reasonable suspicion develops.

Florida v. Royer (1983)

Detention & Movement

Holding: Police cannot move a suspect to another location during a Terry stop without a legitimate law-enforcement purpose. Consent to search is not valid if given while illegally detained.

Curtis v. State (Tex. App. 2006)

Innocent Explanations

Holding: The possibility of an innocent explanation does not deprive an officer of the capacity to have reasonable suspicion. The purpose of an investigation is to resolve ambiguity.

Derichsweiler v. State (Tex. Crim. App. 2011)

No Specific Offense Needed

Holding: An investigative stop can be justified by non-criminal but suspicious behavior. An officer does not need to name a particular offense, but just have RS that the person was about to engage in criminal activity.

City of Indianapolis v. Edmond (2000)

Roadblocks/Checkpoints

Holding: Roadside checkpoints for the primary purpose of general crime control are unconstitutional. Texas law is more restrictive and does NOT allow DWI checkpoints, only DL/insurance checks.

Interactive Scenarios

Scenario 1

An officer on DWI patrol notices a truck with a non-working rear brake light. The officer initiates a stop based on this pretext to conduct a DWI inquiry. The driver, Mr. Smith, immediately becomes irate, accusing the officer of a racially motivated stop. Mr. Smith has not been drinking and poses no safety threat, but is very angry.

Is the initial traffic stop legal? Yes. Under *Whren v. US*, any observed traffic violation (the broken brake light) is a legitimate basis for a stop, making the pretextual nature of the stop constitutional.

Were there signs of racial profiling? Based on the facts given, no. The stop was initiated based on an observable equipment violation, not the driver's race. However, Mr. Smith's perception is critical. The officer should use professional communication skills to de-escalate, explain the reason for the stop clearly (the brake light), and conduct the stop efficiently and professionally to avoid furthering the perception of bias.

Scenario 2

An officer sees an old, battered car in a wealthy neighborhood at night. The officer is suspicious but has no violation. The officer then notices the rear license plate light is out. The officer uses this minor violation to stop the car and, upon approach, sees illegal drugs on the passenger seat.

Was the arrest and seizure of the drugs legal? Yes. The stop was justified by the traffic violation (inoperable license plate light), per *Whren v. US*. The drugs were then discovered in plain view, which is a valid exception to the warrant requirement. The officer's initial suspicion about the car being in a wealthy area is irrelevant to the legality of the stop itself, as long as a valid, objective violation occurred.

Scenario 3

An officer sees an SUV with four young, bearded Middle Eastern men wearing turbans. The vehicle is obeying the speed limit while others are speeding. The officer initiates a stop based on a "feeling" that he should investigate what the men are doing.

Would this be considered "racial profiling?" Yes. The stop is not based on any observed violation of law or articulable facts that would create reasonable suspicion of criminal activity. It is based solely on the occupants' appearance (race/ethnicity/attire) and the officer's "feeling." This is the definition of racial profiling and is an illegal stop.

Scenario 4

A homeowner in an exclusive neighborhood reports a "suspicious" Range Rover occupied by two young Black males with out-of-state plates. The neighborhood has a proactive crime watch. An officer responds and locates the vehicle, which is not violating any laws.

Should the officer stop the vehicle? No. A citizen's call about a "suspicious" vehicle, when the suspicion is based on the race of the occupants and their presence in a certain neighborhood, does not amount to reasonable suspicion. There are no articulable facts that criminal activity is afoot. Initiating a stop would be illegal racial profiling.

How should the officer handle the call? The officer can continue to observe the vehicle. If the officer develops their own independent reasonable suspicion (e.g., observing a traffic violation, seeing the occupants casing houses), then a stop would be justified. The officer could also attempt a voluntary encounter if the vehicle is parked, but must be clear that the occupants are free to leave and not obligated to speak with the officer.
