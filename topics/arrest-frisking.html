Frisking and the Law

A Practical and Legal Guide for Law Enforcement

1. Legal Foundations of Frisking

A frisk (or "pat down") is a limited search for weapons and is strictly governed by the 4th Amendment. Its sole purpose is officer safety. Understanding when a frisk is justified is critical.

Key Principle: A frisk is justified when an officer has Reasonable Suspicion that a person lawfully detained is armed and dangerous.

Articulating Your Justification

Simply stating "for officer safety" is not enough. You must be able to articulate the specific facts and circumstances that made you believe the person could be armed.

Bad Response:

"I did it for officer safety."

Good Response:

"I feared for my safety because it was 4:30 AM, the driver had no ID, the car's window was broken, and in my experience, suspects in stolen vehicles (a felony) are often armed with tools that can be used as weapons."

The "Plain Feel" Doctrine: Minnesota v. Dickerson (1993)

During a lawful frisk for weapons, an officer may seize other contraband only if its identity as contraband is immediately apparent from its touch without further manipulation. If you have to squeeze, slide, or otherwise manipulate an object to determine it is contraband (after you know it's not a weapon), you cannot seize it under this doctrine.

2. Frisking Techniques & Procedures

Proper technique ensures both officer safety and adherence to legal standards. Remember, a frisk is for weapons only and is a pass over the outer clothing.

Frisking Positions
  • Standing: The most common position. The officer should always be behind the suspect in a position of tactical advantage.
  • Kneeling: Suspect is on their knees with legs crossed. Provides good officer control.
  • Prone: For high-risk situations. A suspect in this position would likely be handcuffed *before* any search.
  • "Hands on the wall" or "hands on the head" positions are NOT recommended due to tactical disadvantages.
The "Magic Question"

Before you begin the physical frisk, always ask:

"Before I search you, do you have any weapons or anything that can poke or cut me?"

Physical Frisking Technique
  • Begin from behind the suspect, keeping them off-balance.
  • Control the suspect's hands (e.g., using an interlaced finger grip).
  • Use an open palm to pass over the outer clothing. DO NOT PAT.
  • Use a bladed hand only for sensitive areas.
  • Start with the waistband area, then systematically search the rest of the person's body.
  • Keep your eyes on the subject to observe their movements at all times.

3. Handling Finds, Resistance, & Cuffing

Your response to finding a weapon, or encountering resistance, must be deliberate and tactical.

What if you find an UNDECLARED weapon?

Your response depends on the type of weapon and the overall situation (e.g., if a cover officer is present).

  • Pocket Knife, Screwdriver, Box Cutter, etc.: You may choose to remove and retain it for the remainder of the contact, or leave it and instruct the person not to touch it. Handcuffing for safety is a primary consideration.
  • Firearm: This is a high-risk situation. The immediate tactical goal is to secure the weapon and the suspect. This may involve taking the suspect to a kneeling or prone position to be handcuffed, preferably with the assistance of a cover officer.
What if the suspect resists?
  • If you feel active resistance, your first priority is to regain control. You may need to take control and handcuff immediately.
  • Alternatively, you may need to disengage and transition to an appropriate force option to handle the resistance.
  • Resistance during a lawful search can constitute an offense such as Resisting Arrest/Search (PC 38.03) or Interference with Public Duties (PC 38.15).
How to Cuff from the Frisk Position
  • Pull the suspect's hands back from their body.
  • Move any sleeves with your cuffing hand and then load the cuffs.
  • Apply the bottom cuff first, then the second cuff. This can often be done in one smooth motion.
  • If wrists are too far apart: Apply the first cuff, then use a "peel off" wrist lock grip on the uncuffed hand to bring the hands together safely to apply the second cuff. A cover officer can assist if necessary.
