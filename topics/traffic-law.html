BPOC Objective 22.2 - Define the following terms in the TC

PC 1.07 – Definitions: Bodily Injury

Definition (PC 1.07(8))

Bodily Injury – physical pain, illness, or any impairment of physical condition.

Why is this Important?

This definition is used to enhance offenses in the TRC.

Example:

TRC 545.157 – If a vehicle fails to slow down or move over for a stationary emergency vehicle, the offense is a Class C Misdemeanor (fine only offense), however if the offense results in bodily injury, it becomes a Class B Misdemeanor (a higher fine and jail time).

PC 1.07 – Definitions: Serious Bodily Injury

Definition (PC 1.07(46))

Serious Bodily Injury – bodily injury that creates a substantial risk of death or that causes death, serious permanent disfigurement, or protracted loss or impairment of the function of any bodily member or organ.

Why is this Important?

This definition is used to enhance offenses throughout the TRC.

Example:

TRC 550.021 – Failed to Stop and Render Aid following a crash is a Second Degree Felony if someone suffers serious bodily injury as a result of the crash. Otherwise, the penalties are less severe.

PC 1.07 – Definitions: Individual

Definition (PC 1.07(26))

Individual – means a human being who is alive, including an unborn child at every stage of gestation from fertilization until birth.

Why is this Important?

The inclusion of an unborn child in this definition allows for criminal charges related to the death of an "individual" when an unborn baby is killed in a criminal event such as Murder, Intoxication Manslaughter, Manslaughter, Criminally Negligent Homicide, etc.

Example:

Jim is intoxicated when he runs a stop sign and T-bones another vehicle. A pregnant woman is driving the other vehicle. She survives, however the crash causes the death of her unborn baby. Jim can be prosecuted for Intoxication Manslaughter because the unborn child meets the definition of an "individual" and Jim's intoxication caused the death of that individual.

PC 1.07 – Definitions: Public Place

Definition (PC 1.07(40))

Public Place – any place to which the public or a substantial group of the public has access and includes, but is not limited to, streets, highways, and the common areas of schools, hospitals, apartment houses, office buildings, transport facilities, and shops.

Why is this Important?

Several frequently used offenses must occur in a "public place" before enforcement action can be taken – Driving While Intoxicated, DUI – Minor, Reckless Driving. Additionally, motor vehicle crashes are considered reportable crashes when they occur in a "public place" such as a parking lot.

PC 1.07 – Definitions: Death

Definition (PC 1.07(49))

Death – includes, for an individual who is an unborn child, the failure to be born alive.

Why is this Important?

This definition allows for a person to be held accountable via conventional charges (Murder, Manslaughter, etc.) when their actions cause an unborn child to not be born alive.

PC 19.04 – Manslaughter

BPOC Objective 22.30

Illustrate a working knowledge of the laws, methods, and techniques relative to crash investigation to include when and where the TC applies in crash investigation.

This offense is frequently used in fatal crash investigation. The actions of the person who caused the death must meet the culpable mental state of "recklessly" as defined in PC 6.03.

Recklessly

When a person is aware of but consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk that the circumstances exist or the result will occur. The risk must be of such a nature and degree that its disregard constitutes a gross deviation from the standard of care that an ordinary person would exercise under all the circumstances as viewed from the actor's standpoint.

Apply It:

Jim is traveling 60 MPH in a 60 MPH zone, but the roadway is wet and he encounters a low spot holding water. His vehicle hydroplanes, leaves the roadway, and rolls over several times. Jim's passenger is killed in the crash.

Has Jim committed the offense of Manslaughter?

Yes No

Discussion:

The answer is most likely "No". Although Jim's actions caused a crash that killed his passenger, they were not a "gross deviation" from ordinary standards of care and Jim was not aware that his driving behavior posed a substantial and unjustifiable risk.

PC 19.05 – Criminally Negligent Homicide

This offense is frequently used in fatal crash investigation. The actions of the person who caused the crash/death must meet the culpable mental state of "criminal negligence" as defined in PC 6.03.

Criminal Negligence

When a person ought to be aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk that the circumstances exist or the result will occur. The risk must be of such a nature and degree that the failure to perceive it constitutes a gross deviation from the standard of care that an ordinary person would exercise under all the circumstances as viewed from the actor's standpoint.

Case Law

Queeman v. State, Texas Court of Criminal Appeals – good guidance regarding building a Criminally Negligent Homicide case based on Class C traffic violations.

Apply It:

Jim is traveling 70 MPH in a 60 MPH zone. Jim is running late for an appointment and he is texting his client to reschedule at the time of the crash. It is raining and Jim's tires are extremely worn. The tread depth measures 1/32" after the crash. Jim encounters a low spot holding water. His vehicle hydroplanes, leaves the roadway, and rolls over several times. Jim's passenger is killed in the crash.

Has Jim committed the offense of Criminally Negligent Homicide?

Yes No

Discussion:

These circumstances would likely rise to the level of "criminal negligence". Jim is speeding while texting and driving in the rain on tires with extremely poor tread. Jim should have known that the combination of these elements posed a substantial and unjustifiable risk to his passenger.

Manslaughter vs. Criminally Negligent Homicide

The difference is the culpable mental state:

  • Manslaughter (Recklessly) – aware of the substantial and unjustifiable risk but consciously disregards the risk.
  • CNH (with Criminal Negligence) – ought to be aware of the substantial and unjustifiable risk.

Word Association:

  • Manslaughter → Reckless → Death → 2nd Degree Felony
  • Criminally Negligent Homicide → With Criminal Negligence → Death → State Jail Felony

545.251 – Obedience to Signal Indicating Approach of Train

Simply stated, vehicles must stop and yield to trains and other on-track equipment. The train always has the right-of-way.

Required Stopping Distance:

Stop not closer than 15 feet or farther than 50 feet from the nearest rail when:

  1. Railroad signal warns of approaching train
  2. Crossing gate is lowered or flagger warns of train
  3. Engine within 1,500 feet emits audible signal and is immediate hazard
  4. Train is plainly visible and in hazardous proximity
  5. Required by other traffic control devices

Word Association:

545.251 – Obedience to Signal Indicating Approach of Train

Simply stated, vehicles must stop and yield to trains and other on-track equipment (special equipment operating on the railroad tracks). The train always has the right-of-way.

Required Stopping Distance:

An operator approaching a railroad grade crossing shall stop not closer than 15 feet or farther than 50 feet from the nearest rail if any of the following are present:

Five Situations Requiring Stop:

  1. Railroad signal warns of approaching train (clearly visible)
  2. Crossing gate is lowered or a flagger warns of approach/passage of train
  3. Railroad engine approaching within approximately 1,500 feet emits audible signal and is immediate hazard due to speed/proximity
  4. Approaching railroad train is plainly visible and in hazardous proximity to crossing

An operator required to stop shall remain stopped until permitted to proceed and it is safe to proceed.

472.021 – Tampering with Warning Devices

Offense

It is an offense to tamper with, damage, or remove a barricade, flare pot, sign, flasher signal, or other device warning of construction, repair, or detour on or adjacent to a highway.

Enforcement Notes:

  1. The device must warn of construction, repair, or detour; and
  2. The device must have been placed by the state, a political subdivision, a contractor, or a public utility.

DPS Arrest Title

Tampering with Warning Device

Penalty:

This offense is punishable by a fine between $25 and $1,000 and up to 2 years in county jail.

472.022 – Obeying Warning Signs and Barricades

Offense

It is an offense to disregard a warning sign or drive around a barricade.

Key Definitions:

Barricade

An obstruction: (A) placed on or across a road, street, or highway of this state by the department, a political subdivision of this state, or a contractor or subcontractor constructing or repairing the road, street, or highway under authorization of the department or a political subdivision of this state; and (B) placed to prevent the passage of motor vehicles over the road, street, or highway during construction, repair, or dangerous conditions.

Warning Sign

A signal, marking, or device placed on a barricade or on a road, street, or highway during construction, repair, or dangerous conditions by the department, a political subdivision of this state, or a contractor or subcontractor to warn or regulate motor vehicular traffic. The term includes a flagger deployed on a road, street, or highway by the department, a political subdivision of this state, or a contractor or subcontractor to direct traffic around or on the road, street, or highway during construction, repair, or dangerous conditions.

Enhanced Penalties:

  • Fine can be doubled (to $400) if committed in a construction zone while workers are present
  • Enhanced to a Class B Misdemeanor if the barricade has been placed due to water over the roadway

DPS Arrest Titles:

  • Disregard Warning Signs/Barricades – Class C Misd.
  • Disregard Warning Signs/Barricade-Construction Zone W/Workers – Class C Misd. (double fine)
  • Disregard Warning Signs/Barricades Water Over Road – Class B Misd.

Important Note:

(f) Deferred adjudication and defensive driving do not apply to an offense under this section.

This sign is a warning sign as defined in 472.022(e)(3) and described in the Texas Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (sign CW13-1P). Traveling above 40 MPH constitutes disregarding the warning sign.

502.001 – Key Definitions

Commercial Motor Vehicle (7)

This term is defined numerous times through the TRC, however for registration purposes in Chapter 502, a motor vehicle designed or used primarily to transport property.

Department (11)

For the registration chapter, "department" means the Texas Department of Motor Vehicles.

Farm Semitrailer or Farm Trailer (15)

Designed and used primarily as a farm vehicle.

Farm Tractor (16)

Has the meaning assigned by 541.201 (a motor vehicle designed and used primarily as a farm implement to draw an implement of husbandry, including a plow or a mowing machine.)

Implement of Husbandry (20)

Has the meaning assigned by 541.201 – (A) a vehicle, other than a passenger car or truck, that is designed and adapted for use as a farm implement, machinery, or tool for tilling the soil; (B) a towed vehicle that transports to the field and spreads fertilizer or agricultural chemicals; or (C) a motor vehicle designed and adapted to deliver feed to livestock.

Operate Temporarily on the Highways (30)

Must be going to or coming from a farm and certain other places.

Why is this Important?

This definition is important to understand when considering farm trailer use (502.146). This definition is also utilized in the Texas DMV registration manual – farm tractors and implements of husbandry that are operated "temporarily on the highways" are not subject to registration.

Power Sweeper (33)

Means an implement, with or without motive power, designed for the removal by a broom, vacuum, or regenerative air system of debris, dirt, gravel, litter, or sand from asphaltic concrete or cement concrete surfaces. The term includes a vehicle on which the implement is permanently mounted if the vehicle is used only as a power sweeper.

Why is this Important?

TRC 502.143 states that a power sweeper may not be registered. A vehicle that meets this definition will be operated on highways with no license plate and no registration insignia.

Public Property (36)

Property owned or leased by this state or a political subdivision of this state.

502.005 – Registration of Autocycle

Autocycles are defined identically in 501.008 and 502.005. This definition for "autocycle" is used throughout the TRC.

  1. designed to have when propelled not more than three wheels on the ground;
  2. equipped with seatbelts and a steering mechanism;
  3. equipped with seating that does not require the operator to straddle or sit astride the seat; and
  4. manufactured and certified to comply with federal safety requirements for a motorcycle.

How to Treat an Autocycle:

  • Driver license purposes: treat autocycles like cars (Reference 521.085).
  • Registration purposes: treat autocycles like motorcycles (only one plate is issued and the registration insignia is displayed on the plate).
  • Title purposes: treat autocycles like motorcycles (Reference 501.008).
  • Passenger restrictions: treat autocycles like motorcycles (Reference 545.416(f)).
  • Helmet purposes: treat autocycles like motorcycles unless they are fully or partially enclosed (Reference 661.0015).
  • Seatbelt purposes: treat autocycles like motorcycles (Reference 545.412 – autocycle is not included in the specific definition of "passenger vehicle" used for seatbelts and child safety seats, therefore seatbelts/child safety seats are not required to be used.)

502.095 – One-Trip or 30 Day Trip Permits

These permits are issued to allow operation of motor vehicles that are otherwise unregistered. A vehicle operated beyond the scope of one of these permits is considered to be unregistered.

(c) One-Trip Permit

  • Good for only one trip from point to point. DMV Administrative Code Rule 217.40 states that the trip must either begin or end in Texas.
  • May not be valid for more than 15 days.
  • Used to transport the vehicle only and may not be used to transport passengers or property.
  • Violation of these provisions means the vehicle is no longer covered by the permit and it should be considered to be unregistered.
  • Cost of the One-Trip Permit is $5.
  • A one trip permit does not require a vehicle inspection (reference 548.052).

(d) 30 Day Permit

  • Can only be issued to vehicles with a gross weight or not more than 10,000 pounds unless the vehicle is operated unladen (vehicles with a gross weight of more than 10,000 pounds cannot be operated on a 30 Day Permit while carrying a load – they would need to obtain a 72 or 144-hour permit).
  • Violation of these provisions means the vehicle is no longer covered by the permit and it should be considered to be unregistered.
  • Vehicles operated under a 30 Day Permit are required to be inspected. Failure to be inspected constitutes an offense (Operate Vehicle Without Required Inspection).
  • Cost of the permit is $25.

Display Requirements

One-Trip and 30-Day permits are required to be displayed in the rear window of the vehicle so that the tag is clearly visible and legible, however if the vehicle does not have a rear window the permit must simply be carried somewhere in the vehicle.

(e)(3) Proof of financial responsibility is required for One-Trip and 30-Day Permits.

(f) The registration receipt shall be carried in the vehicle at all times during operation on a One-Trip or 30-Day permit (reference 502.474).

VEHICLES NOT ISSUED REGISTRATION

Although registration is generally required for vehicles to operate on Texas highways, the TRC provides a number of specific exceptions. The Texas Department of Motor Vehicles should not issue registration for the vehicles described in the following sections. Some of these vehicles may still be legally operated on the highway under permits or other circumstances, or they may be issued distinguishing license plates that identify the vehicle but don't constitute full registration.

502.140 – Certain Off-Highway Vehicles

Off-highway vehicle is defined in TRC 551A.001 – this term includes all-terrain vehicles, recreational off-highway vehicles, sand rails, and utility vehicles.

An individual cannot register an off-highway vehicle for operation on a public highway.

The state, county, or a municipality can register an off-highway vehicle for use on a public highway. The vehicle will be issued a normal license plate and registration insignia in these circumstances.

Important: Although an individual is not permitted to register an off-highway vehicle, there are certain circumstances where they would be permitted to operate the vehicle on public highways. These circumstances are described in TRC Chapter 551A. In these circumstances, the off-highway vehicle may have a distinguishing license plate identifying it as an off-highway vehicle, but this does not constitute "registration". The license plate is for identification purposes only.

502.142 – Manufactured Housing

Manufactured housing is not issued registration. If it is moving down the highway, it should be moving under a special permit, not 502.040 registration. The permit will not be displayed on the manufactured house. It will be carried by the operator.

502.143 – Other Vehicles

These vehicles may be encountered operating on public highways, however registration is not issued for them, therefore they are not required to display a license plate, permit, or registration insignia:

  • Power Sweepers
  • Motorized Mobility Devices (electric wheelchairs)
  • Electric Personal Assistive Mobility Devices (Segways)
  • Electric Bicycles
502.144 – Vehicles Operated on Public Highway Separating Real Property

This section allows a property owner to cross the road in an unregistered vehicle if said person owns property on both sides of the road.

502.145 – Vehicles Operated by Certain Non-Residents

This section provides for reciprocity and allows nonresident visitors to Texas from other states to operate vehicles in Texas under their home state registration. Home state registration must be current and valid.

Important: Once a person becomes a resident of Texas reciprocity ends after 30 days and the person's vehicles are subject to Texas registration (502.040).

502.146 – Certain Farm Vehicles and Drilling and Construction Equipment

Vehicles described in this section may be issued distinguishing license plates to identify what they are, however this does not constitute full registration, therefore the vehicles will be limited to specific types of operation.

Farm Trailers

The license plate or registration requirements for farm trailers is dependent on the trailer's gross weight and how the trailer is being used.

Farm trailers are not required to be registered when used "temporarily on the highway" (502.001(30)) to transport agricultural products or supplies to or from the farm or for the purposes of participating in equine activity as long as the trailer's gross weight is not more than 34,000 pounds.

Farm trailers are not required to receive a state inspection regardless of weight.

Farm Trailers with a gross weight of 4,000 pounds or less – no license plate or registration required.

Farm Trailers with a gross weight of more than 4,000 pounds but not more than 34,000 pounds – a Farm Trailer license plate is required, but full registration is not required.

Trailers with a gross weight of more than 34,000 pounds – no longer eligible for Farm Trailer license plates and must have normal trailer registration.

Discussion Points:
  • A trailer used for equine activity or attending livestock shows as defined in the Texas Civil Practice and Remedy Code (87.001) may be registered as a farm trailer. This definition is wide ranging and includes rodeo participation even when the participant receives compensation for participation.
  • Texas DMV Manual Clarification – Trailers with living quarters still qualify for farm trailer license plates if the trailer is used within the normal parameters of a farm trailer (agricultural use or equine activity).

504.945 – Wrong, Fictitious, Altered, or Obscured License Plate

Several arrest titles are derived from 504.945:

Display Wrong License Plate

The arrest title of Display Wrong License Plate would apply to violations of 504.945(a)(1), (a)(2), and (a)(3):

  • (a)(1) – a vehicle displays a license plate issued for a different motor vehicle
  • (a)(2) – a vehicle displays a license plate that is issued for the vehicle under any other law other than by the department (Texas DMV)
  • (a)(3) – a vehicle displays a license plate assigned for a registration period other than the registration period in effect (i.e. the vehicle has been issued new license plates for a new registration period but the owner failed to change the license plates out)

Display Fictitious License Plate

The arrest title of Display Fictitious License Plate would apply to violations of 504.945(a)(4). This offense is a Class B Misdemeanor.

Obscured License Plate

The arrest title of Obscured License Plate would apply to violations of 504.945(a)(5):

(a)(5) – a vehicle displays a license plate that has a blurring or reflective matter that significantly impairs the readability of the name of the state in which the vehicle is registered or the letters or numbers of the license plate number at any time.

Note: This arrest title should not be used for a license plate that is unreadable due to dirt or mutilation. A better fit for that situation is Improperly Placed/Illegible/Unreadable License Plate under 504.943.

Unauthorized Illumination Device/Sticker or Emblem on License Plate

The arrest title of Unauthorized Illumination Device/Sticker or Emblem on License Plate would apply to violations of 504.945(a)(6):

(a)(6) – a vehicle displays a license plate that has an attached illuminated device or sticker, decal, emblem, or other insignia that is not authorized by law and that interferes with the readability of the letters or numbers of the license plate number or the name of the state in which the vehicle is registered.

Note: The illuminated device, sticker, decal, emblem, or other insignia is not a violation unless it interferes with the readability of the license plate number or state name.

Display Unauthorized Coating or Covering on License Plate

The arrest title of Display Unauthorized Coating or Covering on License Plate would apply to violations of 504.945(a)(7):

(a)(7) – a vehicle displays a license plate that has a coating, covering, protective substance, or other material that:

  • (A) distorts angular visibility or detectability
  • (B) alters or obscures one-half or more of the name of the state in which the vehicle is registered
  • (C) alters, covers, or obscures the letters or numbers of the license plate number or the color of the plate

Numerous courts have held that this section can be applied to a license plate bracket as long as the bracket obscures the license plate in the manner described (Martinez v. State, 500 SW 3d 456; Czerwinski v. State, Texas Court of Appeals, 13th Dist., 2018).

A third or subsequent conviction for violating (a)(7)(C) is a Class B Misdemeanor (altering, covering, or obscuring the letters/numbers or color of the license plate).

504.946 – Deceptively Similar License Plate

A deceptively similar license plate is a license plate that was not issued by DMV but a reasonable person would presume it was issued by DMV. These license plates typically bear the correct state and license plate number assigned to that vehicle.

545.060 – Driving on Roadway Laned for Traffic

(a) Lane Positioning

On a roadway divided into two or more clearly marked lanes for traffic in one direction, an operator shall drive as nearly as practical entirely within a single lane.

(b) Lane Changes

An operator may not move from the lane unless the movement can be made safely.

(c) Two-Way Left Turn Lanes

If a roadway has a lane marked for left turns only that may be used by traffic traveling in either direction, an operator may not drive in the lane except when preparing for or making a left turn from the roadway.

Permitted Uses of Left Lanes:

  2. Preparing for a left turn
  3. Main traveled portion of the roadway is not available due to congestion, obstruction, or closure

Drove in Left Lane

545.062 – Following Distance

An operator may not follow another vehicle more closely than is reasonable and prudent, having due regard for the speed of the vehicles, traffic conditions, and the condition of the roadway.

DPS Arrest Title:

Following Too Closely

Factors to Consider:

  • Speed – Higher speeds require more following distance
  • Traffic conditions – Heavy traffic may affect reasonable distance
  • Roadway conditions – Wet, icy, or poor conditions require more space
  • Vehicle types – Large vehicles need more stopping distance

General Guidelines (Not Legal Requirements):

  • 3-Second Rule: Normal conditions, passenger vehicles
  • 4+ Second Rule: Poor weather, large vehicles, or high speeds
  • Commercial vehicles: Often require greater following distances

Apply It:

Enforcement Note:

This is a subjective standard. Consider all factors: speed, weather, traffic, road conditions, and vehicle types when determining if following distance is reasonable and prudent.

545.101 – Turning at Intersection

(a) Right Turns

To make a right turn at an intersection, an operator shall make both the approach for the turn and the turn as closely as practicable to the right-hand curb or edge of the roadway.

(b) Left Turns

To make a left turn at an intersection, an operator shall:

  1. Approach the turn in the extreme left-hand lane lawfully available to traffic moving in the direction of the vehicle
  2. After entering the intersection, make the left turn so as to leave the intersection to the right of the center line of the roadway being entered

(c) Two-Way Left Turn Lanes

Where a special lane for making left turns by drivers proceeding in opposite directions has been indicated by official traffic control devices, a left turn may not be made from any other lane.

(d) Official Traffic Control Device Compliance

An operator may not turn left at an intersection if a traffic control device at the intersection prohibits the turn.

  • Disobey No Left Turn Sign

Scenario 1: A vehicle makes a right turn from the center lane of a three-lane roadway.

Violation: Improper Right Turn

Right turns must be made from as close as practicable to the right curb/edge

Scenario 2: A vehicle approaches in the right lane and makes a left turn from that position.

Violation: Left Turn from Wrong Lane

Left turns must be made from the extreme left-hand lane available

Scenario 3: An intersection has a dedicated left turn lane marked by signs. A vehicle makes a left turn from the through lane.

Violation: Left Turn from Wrong Lane

When special left turn lanes are marked, left turns must be made from that lane only

Scenario 4: A vehicle turns left at an intersection displaying a "No Left Turn" sign.

Violation: Disobey No Left Turn Sign

Traffic control devices prohibiting turns must be obeyed

Right Turn = Right Curb

Left Turn = Left Lane + Right of Center

Enforcement Note:

Watch for proper lane positioning before turns and proper completion path. Special turn lanes override general rules when present.

545.002 – Operator

The term "operator" refers to the person operating the vehicle, but also includes references to the vehicle if the reference imposes a duty or provides a limitation on the movement or other operation of that vehicle.

Many sections in Chapter 545 begin with the words "An operator…" and then describes a prohibited or required action for the vehicle.

Reference 541.001(1-a) – operator: a person who drives or has physical control of a vehicle.

Enforcement Note:

"Operator" can refer to both the person AND the vehicle in Chapter 545 contexts.

545.051 – Driving on Right Side of Roadway

This establishes the general flow of traffic in Texas. Traffic is required to travel on the right half of the roadway unless:

  1. passing another vehicle
  2. safely going around an obstruction
  3. traveling on a roadway divided into three marked lanes, or
  4. traveling on a one way roadway

Traveling on the left side of the roadway outside of these situations constitutes a violation for Wrong Side Road – Not Passing.

(b) Slower Traffic Keep Right

Slower traffic is required to keep in the right hand lane in preference to faster moving vehicles. In order for this offense to take place, there must be other traffic on the highway traveling faster.

There are no requirements for signs to be posted to enforce this violation.

The offense for a violation of this subsection is Slower Vehicle Fail to Keep Right.

(c) Four or More Lane Roadways

Operators may only drive left of the center line of a two-way roadway with 4 or more lanes in one of the following circumstances:

  1. if authorized to do so by law (i.e. a center left turn only lane, reversible lanes, etc.)
  2. to safely go around an obstruction, or
  3. while turning left

Apply It - Multiple Scenarios:

This vehicle is traveling 55 MPH in a 70 MPH zone. Is the driver committing the violation of Slower Vehicle Fail to Keep Right?

Answer: NO - There must be other traffic traveling faster for this violation to occur.

The blue vehicle is traveling 55 MPH in a 70 MPH zone next to the green vehicle. Is the driver committing the violation of Slower Vehicle Fail to Keep Right?

Answer: NO - Both vehicles are traveling the same speed, so there's no faster traffic.

The blue vehicle is traveling 55 MPH in the left lane. The green vehicle is traveling 70 MPH. Is the driver of the blue vehicle committing the violation of Slower Vehicle Fail to Keep Right?

Answer: YES - There is faster traffic (green vehicle at 70 MPH), so the slower blue vehicle should keep right.

The blue vehicle is all alone on the roadway traveling in the left lane. A "Left Lane for Passing Only" sign is posted.

Is the blue vehicle committing a violation for Slower Vehicle Fail to Keep Right?

Answer: NO - No other traffic is present to be "faster."

Is the blue vehicle committing a violation for Drove in Left Lane Not Passing?

Answer: YES - "Left Lane for Passing Only" signs create this specific violation (see 544.011).

Enforcement Note:

For "Slower Vehicle Fail to Keep Right," there MUST be other traffic traveling faster. For "Left Lane for Passing Only" violations, signs must be posted.

545.052 – Driving Past Vehicle Moving in Opposite Direction

When meeting another vehicle on a roadway wide enough for just one vehicle in each direction, operators must yield ½ of the roadway to traffic moving in the opposite direction.

If yielding ½ of the roadway is not possible due to roadway conditions or vehicle size, the vehicle must yield "as much of the roadway as possible."

Meeting Traffic = ½ Roadway

545.053 – Passing to the Left; Return; Being Passed

(a) Requirements for Passing Vehicle

Describes the requirements for the passing vehicle – must pass to the left at a "safe distance" and may not move back to the right until safely clear of the passed vehicle.

Arrest titles from this subsection:

  • Failed to Pass to the Left Safely
  • Cut in After Pass

(b) Requirements for Passed Vehicle

Operators of vehicles being passed must, on audible signal, stay to the right and are not permitted to accelerate until passed.

"Audible signal" is not defined, however this signal must be present in order for an offense to take place by the passed vehicle. The most likely audible signal would be the passing vehicle sounding the horn.

Apply It:

A vehicle passes you but cuts back in too soon, forcing you to brake. What violation has occurred?

Answer: Cut in After Pass - the passing vehicle did not wait until safely clear.

Enforcement Note:

For violations by the passed vehicle, an audible signal from the passing vehicle must be present.

545.054 – Passing to the Left; Safe Distance

Passing to the left is only permitted in legal passing zones and only when it can be done safely. The passing vehicle may not interfere with the operation of the passed vehicle or oncoming traffic and must move back to the right before coming within 200' of oncoming vehicles.

If a lane of opposing traffic is not used during the pass, the passing vehicle must simply return to the right lane as soon as practicable.

Word Association:

Passing to the Left → return before 200 feet of oncoming vehicle

Apply It:

You're passing a vehicle and see an oncoming car 150 feet away. You haven't completed your pass yet. What should you do?

Answer: Abort the pass immediately and return to the right lane - you must return before coming within 200 feet of oncoming traffic.

545.055 – Passing to the Left; Passing Zones

This section is the first of five times a vehicle is not permitted to be left of the center of the roadway (the other four times are described in 545.056, see next section).

This section refers to a "sign or marking" that prohibits being left of center. This is a solid yellow line, also frequently called a "no passing zone". It may also be marked by signs.

Unless a vehicle is making a left turn at an intersection or into a private drive or alley, traveling left of the no passing zone marking is prohibited.

Apply It:

The blue truck began its pass in a legal passing zone but has not quite returned to the right side of the roadway when it reaches the solid yellow line. Is this a violation for Disregard No Passing Zone?

Answer: YES - Once the solid yellow line begins, being left of center is prohibited regardless of where the pass started.

Enforcement Note:

Solid yellow line = no passing zone. Exceptions: left turns at intersections or into private drives/alleys.

545.056 – Driving to Left of Center

A vehicle is not permitted to drive on the left side of the roadway in a no passing zone, as stated in 545.055 (previous section). This section provides four additional times/locations where it is illegal to drive on the left side of the roadway.

These restrictions do not apply when a vehicle is traveling on a one-way roadway or making a left turn.

A vehicle may not drive on the left side of the roadway:

  1. Approaching within 100 feet of an intersection/RR grade crossing in a municipality (in city limits)
  2. Approaching within 100 feet of an intersection/RR grade crossing outside a municipality when marked according to 545.055 (no passing zone). NOTE: The term "shown by a sign or marking" is not referring to a road name sign or other marking. This is a specific reference to a No Passing Zone sign or marking.
  3. Approaching within 100 feet of a bridge, viaduct or tunnel (even if the bridge, viaduct, or tunnel is marked as a passing zone).
  4. While awaiting access at a ferry crossing.

Apply It:

Is this a legal pass?

Answer: IT DEPENDS - If the vehicle is within 100 feet of a bridge, viaduct, or tunnel, it's illegal regardless of passing zone markings. The location relative to these structures determines legality.

Word Association:

100 Feet Rule = Intersections, RR Crossings, Bridges, Viaducts, Tunnels

545.057 – Passing to the Right

This section addresses passing on the right when the passing vehicle is not utilizing an improved shoulder – passing on the improved (paved) shoulder is governed by 545.058 (see next section).

Passing on the right is only permitted when it can be done safely, the vehicle being passed is making a left turn, and the operator of the passing vehicle has unobstructed pavement to the right of the passed vehicle on which to travel.

Passing to the right is never permitted if the passing vehicle leaves pavement.

DPS Arrest Titles:

  • Failed to Pass to Right Safely
  • Illegal Pass on Right

Examples:

Legal pass – front vehicle turning left.

Illegal pass – front vehicle not turning left.

Illegal pass – passing vehicle leaves pavement.

Apply It:

A vehicle passes you on the right shoulder while you're driving straight. What violation has occurred?

Answer: Illegal Pass on Right - the front vehicle was not turning left, which is required for legal right-side passing.

Enforcement Note:

Three requirements for legal right-side passing: 1) Safe, 2) Front vehicle turning left, 3) Unobstructed pavement (no leaving pavement).

545.058 – Driving on Improved Shoulder

Reference 541.302(6) – Definition of improved shoulder: a paved shoulder

RIGHT IMPROVED SHOULDER

Driving on the improved shoulder to the right of the roadway is only permitted if it is done safely and when necessary to accomplish one of the following seven exceptions:

  1. To stop, stand or park.
  2. To accelerate before entering the main traveled traffic lane.
  3. To decelerate before making a right turn.
  4. To pass another vehicle that is slowing or stopped on the main traveled portion of the highway, disabled, or preparing to make a left turn. (Note: there are three separate circumstances described here – passing a vehicle that is slowing or stopped, passing vehicle that is disabled, and passing a vehicle that is preparing to make a left turn).
  5. To allow another vehicle traveling faster to pass.
  6. As permitted or required by an official traffic control device.
  7. To avoid a collision.

Memory Aid - SAD PAPA:

  • S – Stop, stand, or park
  • A – Accelerate before entering
  • D – Decelerate before turning
  • P – Pass a vehicle slowing, stopped, or disabled in main lane
  • A – Allow faster moving vehicle to pass
  • P – Permitted or required by official traffic control device
  • A – Avoid a collision

LEFT IMPROVED SHOULDER ON CONTROLLED ACCESS/LIMITED ACCESS HIGHWAY

Driving on the improved shoulder to the left of the roadway is only permitted in one of the following three times:

  1. To slow or stop when the vehicle is disabled and traffic or other circumstances prohibit the safe movement to the right shoulder.
  2. As permitted or required by an official traffic control device
  3. To avoid a collision

Memory Aid - SPA:

  • S – Slow or stop when disabled
  • P – Permitted or required by an official traffic control device
  • A – Avoid a collision

Exceptions - Prohibitions Do Not Apply To:

  1. authorized emergency vehicles responding to calls
  2. police patrols
  3. bicycles
  4. slow moving vehicles as defined by 547.001 (vehicle designed for maximum speed of 25 MPH or vehicle pulled by an animal)

Case Law – Lothrop v. State

Texas Court of Criminal Appeals, PD-1489-11

(a)(4) "To pass another vehicle that is slowing or stopped" was very broadly interpreted. Also discussed the "when necessary" language in (a) and concluded that it meant "when necessary" to accomplish one of the exceptions.

Lothrop v. State:

The red truck's pass on the right improved shoulder was not illegal because it was passing a vehicle that was "slowing" on the main traveled portion of the roadway and the pass was covered under 545.058(a)(4).

Apply It:

A vehicle uses the right shoulder to pass a slow-moving vehicle that's doing 45 mph in a 70 mph zone. Is this legal?

Answer: YES - Under Lothrop v. State, passing a vehicle that is "slowing" (significantly below speed limit) on the main roadway is covered under 545.058(a)(4).

Enforcement Note:

Remember SAD PAPA for right shoulder and SPA for left shoulder. Lothrop case broadly interprets "slowing" vehicles.

Introduction to CDL Requirements

Why Do We Have CDL Requirements?

Commercial Driver's License (CDL) requirements exist to ensure that drivers of large and potentially dangerous vehicles have the proper training, knowledge, and skills to operate them safely. These requirements help protect both the commercial driver and other road users.

Federal vs. State Authority
  • Federal: Sets minimum standards through the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA)
  • State: Issues licenses and may impose additional requirements beyond federal minimums
  • Texas: Follows federal standards and adds state-specific requirements in Chapter 522

Key Concept:

Size + Danger = Special License Required

Apply It: Why These Requirements Matter

Consider a fully loaded 18-wheeler truck:

  • Can weigh up to 80,000 pounds (40 tons)
  • Takes much longer to stop than a regular car
  • Has significant blind spots
  • Requires special skills for backing, turning, and parking
  • May carry hazardous materials

GCWR 26,001+ lbs with towed unit 10,001+ lbs

16+ passengers or hazardous materials

Basic Requirements for CDL in Texas

  • Must be at least 18 years old (21 for interstate commerce)
  • Must have a valid Texas driver's license
  • Must pass written knowledge tests
  • Must pass skills (road) test
  • Must meet medical requirements
  • Must provide required documentation

544.007 – Traffic Control Signals in General

This section governs traffic control signals (red, yellow, and green lights). Drivers have specific obligations depending on the color of signal they are facing:

(b) Circular Green Signal

Vehicle may proceed after yielding ROW to other vehicles and pedestrians lawfully in the intersection or adjacent crosswalk when the green signal is exhibited.

(c) Green Arrow

Vehicle may enter the intersection in the direction indicated, however must still yield ROW to other vehicles and pedestrians lawfully in the intersection or adjacent crosswalk when the green signal is exhibited.

(d) Steady Red Signal

Vehicle must stop at one of two places:

  1. a clearly marked stop line; or
  2. in the absence of a clearly marked stop line, before entering the crosswalk.

After stopping, a vehicle may turn right on a steady red signal after yielding ROW. A vehicle may also turn left after stopping if both roadways are one way roadways.

(e) Steady Yellow Signal

The vehicle is warned that movement authorized by a green signal is being terminated or a red signal is to be given.

Special Situations:

  • (g) Signal at place other than intersection – if a traffic control signal is placed in a location other than at an intersection and a clearly marked stop line is not provided, the stop for a red signal is required to be made at the signal.
  • (i) Signal present but not displaying an indication – if a traffic control signal does not display an indication in any of the signal heads (i.e. the signal is not working due to power loss or some other problem), traffic is required to treat the signal as if it were a stop sign.

Exceptions to (i):

  • Pedestrian hybrid beacon – no stop required if no signal is displayed
  • Freeway entrance ramp control signal – no stop required if no signal is displayed

General Rule

An operator may not stop, stand, or park a vehicle except when necessary to avoid conflict with other traffic or to comply with the directions of a police officer or official traffic control device.

  2. In front of a public or private driveway
  3. Within an intersection
  4. Within 15 feet of a fire hydrant
  5. On a crosswalk
  6. Within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection
  7. Within 30 feet of a flashing signal, stop sign, or traffic control signal located at the side of a roadway
  8. Between a safety zone and the adjacent curb or within 30 feet of points on the curb immediately opposite the ends of a safety zone (unless a different length is indicated by signs or markings)
  9. Within 20 feet of the driveway entrance to any fire station and on the side of a street opposite the entrance to any fire station within 75 feet of the entrance (when properly signed)
  10. Alongside or opposite any street excavation or obstruction when stopping, standing, or parking would obstruct traffic
  11. On the roadway side of any vehicle stopped or parked at the edge or curb of a street (double parking)
  12. On a bridge or other elevated structure on a highway or within a highway tunnel
  13. On railroad tracks
  14. Where an official sign prohibits stopping

  • Prohibited Parking (general violations)
  • Blocking Driveway (subsection (a)(2))
  • Parking Too Close to Fire Hydrant (subsection (a)(4))
  • Double Parking (subsection (a)(11))

545.304 – Stopping, Standing, or Parking Outside a Business or Residence District

Outside a business or residence district, an operator may not stop, stand, or park a vehicle on the main-traveled part of a highway unless:

  1. It is necessary to avoid conflict with other traffic, or
  2. To comply with the directions of a police officer or official traffic control device

The territory contiguous to and including a highway when within any 600 feet along the highway there are buildings in use for business or industrial purposes, including but not limited to hotels, banks, or office buildings, that occupy at least 300 feet of frontage on one side or 300 feet collectively on both sides of the highway.

The territory contiguous to and including a highway, not comprising a business district, when the property on the highway for a distance of 300 feet or more is in the main improved with residences or residences and buildings in use for business.

  2. Poses a threat to public safety

545.308 – Presumption

Legal Presumption

In the prosecution of an offense under this subchapter, proof that a person was driving a vehicle or was the vehicle's registered owner when an offense occurred establishes a rebuttable presumption that the person committed the offense.

Key Legal Concepts:

Rebuttable Presumption

A legal assumption that can be overcome with evidence to the contrary. The burden shifts to the defendant to prove they did not commit the offense.

Application

This applies to offenses under Subchapter C (Rules of the Road) of Chapter 545.

Apply It - Legal Scenario:

A registered owner receives a citation in the mail for a red light violation captured on camera. The owner claims they were not driving. What is the legal effect?

Answer: The presumption establishes that the registered owner committed the offense, but they can present evidence (testimony, alibi, etc.) to rebut this presumption and prove someone else was driving.

Enforcement Note:

This provision supports automated enforcement systems and assists in prosecution when driver identity is disputed.

545.351 – Maximum Speed Requirement

Basic Speed Rule

An operator may not drive at a speed greater than is reasonable and prudent under the circumstances then existing.

Factors to Consider:

  • Weather conditions
  • Traffic conditions
  • Road conditions
  • Visibility
  • Pedestrian and other traffic

DPS Arrest Title:

Driving at Unreasonable Speed

Apply It - Weather Scenario:

During heavy rain with reduced visibility, a driver travels 55 mph in a 65 mph zone but loses control due to the conditions. Can they be cited under 545.351?

Answer: YES - Even though under the posted limit, 55 mph may not be reasonable and prudent given the weather conditions. Speed must be appropriate for circumstances, not just under the limit.

Enforcement Note:

Document environmental conditions thoroughly. This charge applies when speed is inappropriate for conditions, regardless of posted limits.

545.352 – Prima Facie Speed Limits

Prima Facie Speed Limits (Unless Otherwise Posted)

  • 30 mph - in an urban district on a street other than an alley
  • 15 mph - in an alley

Legal Effect of Prima Facie Limits

Prima facie evidence that a speed in excess of the limits is unreasonable and imprudent, but this evidence may be rebutted by competent evidence that the speed was reasonable and prudent.

Memory Aid:

"30-15 Rule" - 30 mph on urban streets, 15 mph in alleys

Apply It - Prima Facie Scenario:

A driver travels 35 mph on an urban street with no posted speed limit. They argue the speed was safe for conditions. What is the legal effect?

Answer: Prima facie evidence of unreasonable speed (exceeding 30 mph default), but the driver can present evidence that 35 mph was reasonable for the specific conditions.

545.353 – Posted Speed Limits

Authority to Post Speed Limits

The Texas Transportation Commission, for highways and roads in the state highway system, and local authorities, for highways under their jurisdiction, may set speed limits.

Requirements for Posted Limits

  • Must be determined based on an engineering and traffic investigation
  • Must consider safety of all users
  • Signs must be posted to give notice

DPS Arrest Title:

Speeding or Speed Over Limit

Enforcement Note:

Verify posted speed limit signs are properly placed and visible. Engineering studies support the validity of posted limits.

545.354 – Minimum Speed Regulations

General Rule

An operator may not drive so slowly as to impede the normal and reasonable movement of traffic, except when reduced speed is necessary for safe operation or in compliance with law.

Posted Minimum Speed Limits

When the Texas Transportation Commission or a local authority determines that slow speeds on a highway consistently impede the normal and reasonable movement of traffic, the authority may post minimum speed limits.

DPS Arrest Title:

Impeding Traffic or Driving Under Minimum Speed

Apply It - Slow Driver Scenario:

On a highway with a posted minimum speed of 45 mph, a driver travels 40 mph due to an uncertain GPS signal, causing other vehicles to change lanes frequently. Is this a violation?

Answer: YES - Driving below the posted minimum speed limit violates 545.354, regardless of the driver's reason (unless for safety or legal compliance).

Enforcement Note:

Consider traffic conditions and driver's reason for slow speed. Exception exists for safety or legal compliance.

545.355 – Speed Limit Exception for Emergencies

Emergency Vehicle Exception

The speed limitations in this subchapter do not apply to:

  1. Authorized emergency vehicle responding to a call
  2. Police patrol vehicle when responding to an emergency call or pursuing a suspected violator
  3. Ambulance when responding to an emergency call

Required Conditions

The operator of the emergency vehicle must drive with due regard for the safety of all persons using the highway.

Apply It - Emergency Response:

A police officer responding to a burglary in progress drives 75 mph in a 55 mph zone without lights or siren. The officer causes a minor accident. Are they protected by 545.355?

Answer: Potentially NO - While speed limits don't apply, the officer must drive with "due regard for safety." Driving at high speed without emergency equipment activated may violate the due regard standard.

Enforcement Note:

"Due regard for safety" is the key standard. Emergency vehicles are not exempt from all traffic laws, just speed limits when properly responding.

545.356 – Speed Limit Exception for Certain Vehicles

Reduced Speed Requirements

A person may not operate the following vehicles at a speed in excess of the posted speed limit or 55 mph, whichever is lower:

  • A school bus
  • A vehicle towing another vehicle
  • A truck with a manufacturer's rated carrying capacity of at least one-half ton that is transporting workers to or from a job site
  • A commercial motor vehicle

Special Exception

This section does not apply to a commercial motor vehicle for which a speed limit is established under Subsection (g) or Subchapter K.

Memory Aid:

"55 or Posted - Whichever is LOWER" for special vehicles

Apply It - Commercial Vehicle Scenario:

A school bus travels 60 mph in a 65 mph zone. Is this a violation?

Answer: YES - School buses are limited to 55 mph or the posted limit, whichever is lower. In a 65 mph zone, the school bus limit is 55 mph.

Enforcement Note:

Check vehicle type carefully. CMV drivers should have appropriate licenses and follow federal regulations.

545.365 – Speed Limit in Construction or Maintenance Work Zone

Construction Zone Speed Limits

A local authority or the Texas Department of Transportation may prescribe a special speed limit of 75 mph or less in a construction or maintenance work zone.

Required Signage

The special speed limit must be posted by a sign that:

  • Indicates the beginning and end of the work zone
  • States the special speed limit

Enhanced Penalties

When workers are present, fines for speeding violations may be doubled.

Apply It - Work Zone Scenario:

A driver travels 65 mph in a construction zone posted at 55 mph. No workers are present at the time. What speed violation occurred?

Answer: Regular speeding violation (10 mph over). Enhanced penalties only apply when workers are present, but the posted work zone speed limit is still enforceable.

Enforcement Note:

Document whether workers are present for enhanced penalties. Verify proper signage marking work zone boundaries.

Memory Aid:

"Workers Present = Double Fine"

545.401 – Reckless Driving

Definition

A person commits this offense if they operate a vehicle in willful or wanton disregard for the safety of persons or property.

Unique Enforcement Authority

This is one of the few TRC violations that can be enforced in areas other than on public highways. Reckless Driving can be filed in parking lots or other public places as long as the officer can articulate the willful or wanton disregard for safety.

Penalties and Arrest Requirements

  • Fine of up to $200
  • Up to 30 days in jail
  • Class B Misdemeanor (PC 12.41)
  • ARREST REQUIRED - Do not cite and release

Case Law - White v. State (647 S.W.2d 751)

The appeals court established a definition for willful or wanton disregard: "the deliberate conscious indifference to the safety of others."

Apply It - Reckless Driving Scenarios:

Scenario 1: A driver speeds through a crowded parking lot at 50 mph, weaving between pedestrians and parked cars.

Analysis: YES - Reckless driving. Shows willful disregard for safety in non-highway location.

Scenario 2: A driver accidentally runs a red light due to distraction.

Analysis: NO - Not reckless driving. Lacks willful or wanton disregard element.

Enforcement Note:

Must be able to articulate "deliberate conscious indifference to safety." Document specific behaviors that demonstrate willful disregard.

545.402 – Moving a Parked Vehicle

Requirement

Movement of a stopped, standing, or parked vehicle cannot begin until such movement can be made safely.

DPS Arrest Title

Unsafe Start from Stopped, Standing, or Parked Position

Apply It:

A driver parallel parked on a busy street opens their door into traffic and pulls out without checking for oncoming vehicles, causing another driver to swerve. Violation?

Answer: YES - Failed to ensure movement could be made safely before beginning to move from parked position.

545.403 – Driving Through Safety Zone

Prohibition

An operator may not drive through or in a safety zone.

Definition (TRC 541.302(12))

"Safety zone" means the area in a roadway officially designated for exclusive pedestrian use and that is protected or so marked or indicated by adequate signs as to be plainly visible at all times while so designated.

Apply It:

A driver cuts through a clearly marked pedestrian island to avoid waiting at a traffic light. Violation?

Answer: YES - Driving through a safety zone designated for exclusive pedestrian use.

545.404 – Unattended Motor Vehicle

Requirements Before Leaving Vehicle Unattended

  1. Stop the engine
  2. Lock the ignition
  3. Remove the key from the ignition
  4. Set the parking brake
  5. Turn front wheels to the curb if standing on a grade

Important Limitation

The offenses in Subtitle C only apply to a vehicle on a highway (542.001). Vehicles left unattended in parking lots or residential driveways are not regulated by this section.

Memory Aid - "SLRPW":

Stop engine, Lock ignition, Remove key, Parking brake, Wheels to curb (if on grade)

Apply It:

A driver parks on a steep hill on a public street, sets the parking brake, but leaves the engine running and wheels pointing straight. Violation?

Answer: YES - Failed to stop engine and turn wheels to curb when on a grade.

545.407 – Following or Obstructing Fire Apparatus or Ambulance

Fire Apparatus Rules

  • May not follow closer than 500 feet a fire apparatus responding to an alarm
  • May not drive into or park a vehicle in the block where the fire apparatus has stopped for an alarm call

Ambulance Rules

  • May not follow closer than 500 feet an ambulance that is flashing red lights
  • May not drive or park in an area an ambulance has been summoned in a manner intended to interfere with arrival or departure

Important Note

This law is specific to fire trucks and ambulances. There is no mention of police vehicles and the TRC does not prescribe a specific following distance for responding police vehicles or other authorized emergency vehicles.

Memory Aid:

"500 Feet from Fire and Flashing" - Both fire apparatus and flashing ambulances require 500-foot following distance

Apply It:

A driver follows a fire truck responding to a call at a distance of 300 feet. The fire truck is using emergency lights and sirens. Violation?

Answer: YES - Must maintain 500 feet following distance from fire apparatus responding to alarm.

545.408 – Crossing Fire Hose

Prohibition

It is unlawful to cross a firehose without consent from the department official in command when the hose is intended for use at a fire or alarm of fire.

Apply It:

During a structure fire, a driver drives over fire hoses laid across the street without getting permission from the fire chief. Violation?

Answer: YES - Crossed fire hose without consent from department official in command.

Enforcement Note:

Must be fire hose "intended for use at a fire or alarm of fire." Training exercises or non-emergency use may not qualify.

545.409 – Drawbars and Trailer Hitches; Saddle-Mount Towing

Drawbar Requirements

  • Must be strong enough to pull all the weight drawn
  • Must not be longer than 15 feet

Chain, Rope, or Cable Towing

If towing with a chain, rope, or cable, a white flag at least 12 inches square must be displayed on the rope, cable, or chain.

Saddle-Mount Method

No more than three motor vehicles may be attached to the towing vehicle by the saddle-mount method.

Word Association:

Towing → Not longer than 15 feet → White flag on chain or cable

Correct Answer: C) Both A and B - Chain exceeds 15-foot limit and lacks required white flag.

545.410 – Towing Safety Chains

When Required

Towing safety chains are required when a passenger car or light truck is towing a trailer or another vehicle.

Passenger car: Motor vehicle (not motorcycle) used to transport persons, designed for 10 or fewer passengers including operator

Light truck: Truck with manufacturer's rated carrying capacity of 2,000 pounds or less

Four Exceptions (No Safety Chains Required)

  1. Trailers used for agricultural purposes
  2. Vehicles operated under Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations (most CMVs)
  3. Trailers with safety chains installed prior to 8/18/1994
  4. Fifth wheel or gooseneck trailers (admin code exception)

Administrative Code Specifications (TAC 37.1.21.5)

  1. Two separate chains of equal length on both sides
  2. May not be directly welded - must use bolts, pins, or other methods
  3. Must be long enough to permit free turning
  4. May not contact roadway surface during operation
  5. Must prevent tow-bar from dropping if units separate
  6. Must be strong enough to prevent separation

Apply It:

A pickup truck (light truck) is towing a utility trailer with a proper hitch but no safety chains. The trailer is used to haul lawn equipment for a landscaping business. Violation?

Answer: YES - Light truck towing trailer requires safety chains. Agricultural use exception doesn't apply to commercial landscaping.

Memory Aid - "Two Strong Chains Never Drop":

Two chains, Strong enough, Never touch ground, Drop prevention

545.412 – Child Safety Seat

Age and Height Requirements

Child Safety Seats are required for children who are younger than 8 years of age unless the child is taller than 4'9".

• If a child is taller than 4'9", a child safety seat is not required regardless of age

• If a child is 8 years or older, a child safety seat is not required regardless of height

Vehicle Types Covered

"Passenger vehicle" includes: passenger car, light truck, sport utility vehicle, passenger van (15 or fewer passengers), truck, or truck tractor.

Two Exceptions (No Safety Seat Required)

  1. Transporting passengers for hire (taxis, limos, Uber, etc.)
  2. All seating positions with safety systems are occupied

Apply It - Child Safety Seat Scenarios:

Scenario 1: 6-year-old child, 4'8" tall, sitting in regular seat with seat belt

Violation: YES - Under 8 and under 4'9", needs child safety seat

Scenario 2: 7-year-old child, 4'10" tall, using seat belt only

Violation: NO - Taller than 4'9", safety seat not required

Scenario 3: 5-year-old in taxi without child seat

Violation: NO - Exception for passengers for hire

Memory Aid:

"Under 8 AND Under 4'9" = Child Seat Required"

545.413 – Safety Belts; Offense

General Requirements

  • All persons in seats equipped with safety belts must be secured
  • Applies to same "passenger vehicles" as child safety seats
  • 15 years or older: Person commits violation if unrestrained
  • School bus operators: Must wear safety belt

Driver Responsibility

If the unsecured person is a passenger younger than 17 years, the driver commits a violation for having an unrestrained passenger in the vehicle.

Important Exceptions

  1. Medical reasons: Person has written statement from licensed physician that wearing safety belt would be harmful
  2. Vehicles for hire: Taxicabs, limousines, ride-sharing services
  3. Rural delivery: USPS rural letter carrier on duty
  4. Newspaper delivery: Person delivering newspapers from vehicle
  5. Frequent stops: Person performing duties that require frequent stops and leaving the vehicle
  6. Utility/garbage trucks: Operators of certain commercial vehicles making frequent stops

Apply It - Safety Belt Scenarios:

Scenario 1: 16-year-old passenger not wearing seat belt in front seat

Violation: Driver cited - Responsible for passengers under 17

Scenario 2: 18-year-old passenger not wearing seat belt

Violation: Passenger cited - 15+ years old, person commits violation

Scenario 3: Taxi passenger not wearing seat belt

Violation: NO - Exception for vehicles for hire

Memory Aid:

"15+ = Self, Under 17 = Driver"

545.414 – Riding in Open Beds, Boats, and Trailers

General Prohibition

A person may not operate an open-bed pickup truck, open flatbed truck, or open flatbed trailer when a child younger than 18 years is occupying the bed of the truck or trailer.

Seven Exceptions (Under 18 May Ride in Open Bed)

  1. Supervised by adult: Child supervised by adult in same bed area
  2. Emergency: Secured due to emergency or medical necessity
  3. Hayride/parade: Hayride, parade, or other recreational event
  4. Farm/ranch work: Farm or ranch in rural area where truck operated for agricultural purposes
  5. Beach driving: Beach driving (designated areas)
  6. Hunting/camping: Hunting, fishing, camping, or other recreational activity
  7. Short distance transport: Transportation for short distances at low speeds for work purposes

Boat and Trailer Regulations

  • Boat operation: No person may ride in or on a boat or personal watercraft being transported on a trailer
  • House trailer: No person may ride in a house trailer being moved on a highway

Apply It:

A 17-year-old is riding in the bed of a pickup truck on a highway while the family drives to a camping site 50 miles away. Violation?

Answer: NO - Falls under camping/recreational activity exception.

Supervised, Hayride, Agriculture, Parade, Emergency, Work transport, Beach/hunting
