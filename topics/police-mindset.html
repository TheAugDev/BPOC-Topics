Police Mindset & Tactics

A Guide to Mental Preparation and Situational Awareness

1. The Learning Mindset: Expectations & Growth

Success in law enforcement training and practice requires a specific mindset geared towards continuous growth. This begins with understanding the "Learning Circle," where individuals are pushed beyond their comfort zones to expand their knowledge, skills, and abilities.

The Role of Mistakes

Mistakes are a necessary part of learning. However, it's crucial to distinguish between productive and counterproductive mistakes:

  • Working Mistakes: These happen when you are pushing your limits and trying to do the right thing for the right reason. They are essential for growth.
  • "Braking" Mistakes: These are counterproductive and stem from a lack of focus, not following instructions, or repeating the same error.

"You earned the right to be in this academy class. Now, you must earn the right to stay."

2. Critical Decision-Making (CDM) & W.I.N.

The CDM provides an organized method for making choices in any situation. At its heart is the question: "What's Important Now?" (W.I.N.). After every decision, your next question should be W.I.N.?

The Spinning Model (CDM)
  • Core: The center of the model is your agency's moral compass—its mission, values, and ethics. No step in the CDM should ever contradict this core.
  • Information Collection: The first step is to gather information about the situation.
  • Threat/Risk Assessment: Analyze the information to assess threats and risks.
  • Police Powers & Agency Policy: Consider your legal authority and agency rules.
  • Actions & Behaviors: Choose and execute your actions based on the previous steps.
  • After Action Review: Re-evaluate the outcome. This feeds back into information collection, creating a continuous "spinning" loop of decision-making.
Implicit Bias

Be aware of implicit bias—the subconscious attitudes or stereotypes that can affect your understanding, decisions, and actions. These biases can influence split-second decision-making and must be actively managed to ensure fair and objective policing.

3. Mental Preparation: The #1 Survival Factor

Mental conditioning and preparedness are the most important factors in officer survival. The transition from a civilian to a police mindset requires deliberate effort and practice.

The Conscious vs. Sub-Conscious Mind

Conscious Mind

  • Logical & Analytical
  • Reasoning & Rational
  • Short-term memory
  • Willpower

Sub-Conscious Mind

  • Intuition ("Gut Feeling")
  • Emotions
  • Long-term memory
  • Protective Instincts

Effective policing requires leveraging both. Your "gut instinct" is often your sub-conscious mind recognizing patterns from your training and experience much faster than your conscious mind can process them.

Imagery: Mental Rehearsal

Imagery is the act of creating or re-creating an experience in the mind. The purpose is to focus and direct the imagination to enhance performance.

Brain scan studies show that the same neurons fire during memory recollection as during the original experience. This means mental rehearsal—vividly imagining yourself successfully performing a task—builds and strengthens the neural pathways needed for high-stress performance. Elite performers, from Blue Angels pilots to professional athletes, use this technique extensively.

4. Safety & Situational Awareness

Situational Awareness is the intentional direction of attention to your environment to understand its significance and project future consequences. It is about moving from being a passive observer ("Zoo Lion") to an active participant ("Hunter").

The Color Codes of Awareness

  • White: Relaxed and unaware. You are a potential victim.
  • Yellow: Alert to your surroundings. You are paying attention to noises, movement, and people. This is the lifetime commitment for a peace officer.
  • Orange: Your senses are alerted to a specific potential threat. You are formulating a plan.
  • Red: Taking action. Fighting, running, chasing.
  • Black: Panic. Freezing or irrational behavior, often due to a lack of preparation.

The more time you spend in Condition White, the higher your risk. The more time in Yellow and Orange, the greater your chance of survival.

Reducing Lag Time

Studies show that action is faster than reaction. An attacker can fire a concealed weapon in a fraction of the time it takes an officer to react to the stimulus. We give ourselves more time through:

Proper Tactics
Distance
Barriers / Cover

The 4 Personal Contact Risk Zones (Continual Scan)

You must continually scan the four risk zones of any person you contact:

  1. Face & Chest (for emotional state and breathing changes)
  2. Hands (the #1 threat delivery system)
  3. Waistband Area
  4. Immediate Area & Stance (their position and body language)

5. Officer Mindset & Field Interview Tactics

An officer must be able to fluidly shift between two primary operational mindsets based on the situation's safety level.

Verbal Persuasion Mode

Used when the situation is SAFE and you can use communication to achieve voluntary compliance.

  • 7-Step Professional Contact
  • PACE & LEAPS
  • Deflection of Verbal Abuse

Recognize-React Mode

Used when a SAFE violation occurs. You must react to restore safety before returning to verbal persuasion.

  • Security Risk (e.g., suspect reaches for waistband)
  • Attack
  • Flight / Fleeing
  • Excessive Repetition (refusing lawful commands)

Your reaction involves using tactics like distance, cover, commands, and appropriate force options.

Contact & Cover Principle

Primary Officer's Role

The primary officer is in charge of the call and conducts ALL business. This includes:

  • Interviewing all parties (victim, suspect, witness).
  • Running computer checks.
  • Delegating tasks to other officers.
  • Making arrest decisions.
Backup Officer's Role

The backup officer is NOT a spectator. Their sole responsibility is the safety and security of the scene. This includes:

  • Taking a position of tactical advantage (e.g., the "L" position) to observe all subjects.
  • Communicating any threat observations to the primary officer.
  • Addressing any threats directly.
  • Avoiding lengthy conversations that detract from safety awareness.

Searching Procedures

A search incidental to a lawful arrest is a full search of the person and any bags/containers in their possession. The goal is to find weapons or contraband, ensure officer and facility safety, and safeguard prisoner property.

  • Always ask the "magic question" first: "Before I search, do you have ANY weapons or anything that can poke me?"
  • Search from a position of control and tactical advantage (behind the suspect).
  • Always start with the back waistband.
  • Search slowly and methodically, using an open hand to pass over the clothing. Do NOT pat.
