Criminal Investigation

Interrogation, Rights, & Timelines

Tactics for Interviewing Suspects

  • DO: Eliminate physical barriers and distractions.
  • DO: Know what information you have and what you need to obtain.
  • DO: Allow the suspect to tell their complete story at least once before confronting discrepancies.
  • DON'T: Interrupt them as they talk; they become more comfortable and share more.
  • DON'T: Ask rapid-fire questions; allow them time to answer.
  • DON'T: Ask leading or suggestive questions.
  • DON'T: Supply answers within your questions.
  • This is the most important phase. Spend plenty of time on it.
  • Get to know the subject by asking questions unrelated to the case.
  • Be sincere and find a way to empathize. Sincerity breeds trust.
  • Don't be judgmental. Act as though any admission was expected. A suspect's biggest fear is being judged as worthless.
  • Make it a conversation to make them feel comfortable.
  • Let Them Commit: Don't be in a hurry to confront lies. Let them commit to a story first. Lock them into an account so you can come back with direct questions on inconsistencies.
  • Concentrate on Motives: Offer a possible motive that allows a suspect to confess while saving face. Watch for signs of agreement.
  • Be Confident: When you confront them, you must be confident and definite. The slightest hint of doubt makes a confession unlikely.
  • Stay in Control: Control your emotions and the conversation. Be patient.
  • Leave Them an Out: Allow them a face-saving way to confess if possible.
  • Avoid Threats/Bluffing: Unless you are very confident of success.
  • Time is limited, but interviews should not be rushed.
  • Establishing rapport is still important. Use previous relationships if you have them.
  • Point out when their story does not make sense.
  • If there are multiple suspects, use them against one another.
  • Use the same techniques as a detective, just with a shorter rapport-building time.

Constitutional Rights & Statements

When Does Miranda Apply?

The 5th Amendment protects against self-incrimination. Miranda Warnings are required when two factors are present:

1. CUSTODY
The person is under arrest or deprived of freedom in any significant way.
+
2. INTERROGATION
The person is being questioned about the offense.
  • You have the right to remain silent and not make any statement at all.
  • Any statement you make may be used as evidence against you in court.
  • You have the right to have a lawyer present to advise you prior to and during any questioning.
  • If you are unable to employ a lawyer, you have the right to have a lawyer appointed.
  • You have the right to terminate the interview at any time.

If a suspect invokes their right to an attorney, all questioning must stop.

Police cannot re-initiate contact until:

  • An attorney is present, OR
  • There is at least a 14-day break in Miranda custody, OR
  • The suspect re-initiates contact with law enforcement.

CCP 38.21: A statement may be used if it was made freely and voluntarily, without compulsion or persuasion.

CCP 38.22: For a written statement from custodial interrogation to be admissible, it must show on its face that the Miranda warning was given and the rights were knowingly, intelligently, and voluntarily waived.

Oral or sign language statements from custodial interrogation must be electronically recorded (video/audio), include the Miranda warning on the recording, and all material voices must be identified.

An officer may arrest without a warrant under certain conditions:

  • CCP 14.03: A person makes an admissible statement to the officer that establishes probable cause they committed a felony.
  • CCP 14.04: An officer has satisfactory proof from a credible person that a felony has been committed, AND the offender is about to escape, so there is no time to get a warrant.

Constructing a Case Timeline

Why Build a Timeline?

A timeline helps organize important case events. It allows you to visualize the sequence of events, identify gaps or contradictions in stories, and structure your investigation and final report.

How to Construct a Timeline

  • Note Important Events: Start by noting all important events with any known times.
  • Place Unknowns: Place events with unknown times logically between the known ones.
  • Plot Investigation Efforts: Add your own actions (arrival, interviews, evidence collection) to the timeline.
  • Use a Vertical Structure: A vertical plot of events allows for more detailed notes next to each entry, covering the Who, What, Where, When, and How/Why for each point in time.

Example Timeline Structure

TIME
EVENT (Who, What, Where, When, How/Why)
17:05
Call received from reporter (Jane Doe) re: family fight at 123 Main St.
17:10
Officer Smith arrives on scene.
17:18
Officer Smith separates parties; begins interview with victim (Mary Roe).
17:39
Suspect (John Roe) placed under arrest for Assault FV.

Practical Scenarios

Role Play Scenario: Domestic Dispute

Officers are dispatched to a family fight in progress. The reporter is a family member at a party who says the male and female residents are in a physical fight. No weapons mentioned.

  • Witness/Reporter: You are a family member. You only saw arguing, but it escalated when they went inside. You heard the female yell "stop!" and "let me go!" You called police due to their history of fighting. Be cooperative.
  • Witness 2: You are a friend of the female from work. You did not see anything and do not want to be involved. Only answer direct questions.
  • Complainant (Female): You and your husband were arguing. In the kitchen, he tried to block you from leaving. You admit nothing physical happened today, despite past incidents. You don't know why police were called.
  • Possible Offender (Male): You were arguing about text messages on her phone. In the kitchen, you reached for her phone and she grabbed for it, accidentally scratching you in the process.

Officer's Task: Control the scene, separate witnesses, compare stories, and determine if an offense occurred and if an arrest is necessary. Apply interview techniques to get past the initial stories and find the truth.

Discussion Scenario: "What About Frank?"

At the start of an interview, you ask Frank if he is injured. He tells you no, and you do not notice any injuries on him during the interview.

Task: After watching an (implied) video of Frank's interview, what follow-up questions should have been asked? Were there open ends in his story that need to be addressed? How does his account compare to other evidence?
