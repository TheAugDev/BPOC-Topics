The 8 Criteria & Writing Skills

Exact conformity to truth. Errors cause false facts to become "legal truth." Be fair and report all sides and accounts, even if they contradict. Document facts that might explain contradictions.

No unnecessary details or wordiness. Avoid "play-by-play" reports of normal routines or unimportant actions. However, do not sacrifice clarity, completeness, or accuracy just to be brief. Some situations (deception, use of force) require more context.

All relevant facts included and all form fields filled. Good contact info is crucial for follow-ups. Detail any aliases used and use proper codes for suspects, victims, etc.

Only one interpretation is possible. The goal is to inform, not entertain. Typically written in first person, past tense. Use last names, not role terms like "Suspect 1," unless multiple people share a last name.

Must be readable by others, whether handwritten or typed.

Report facts without bias, distortion, or personal opinions. Describe facts, don't translate them. Any opinions MUST be labeled as such. Lead the reader to a conclusion with facts; don't state the conclusion for them.

Follows rules of grammar for clarity and professionalism.

Proper spelling is essential for professionalism and clarity.

Instead of using shortcut words or conclusions, describe the specific actions and facts that led you to that conclusion.

  • Instead of "Injury": Describe the cut, red mark, scrape, burn, stab/gunshot wound.
  • Instead of "Nervous": Describe the pacing, running hands through hair, avoiding eye contact, trembling voice.
  • Instead of "Signs of a struggle": Describe the knocked over chair, picture askew, scuff marks on the wall, broken lamp, torn clothing.
  • Instead of "Suspicious": Describe the specific actions that were suspicious. "When I saw him, it was 99 degrees outside, and he was running from the scene, wearing a ski mask and socks on his hands."

An officer's ability to be descriptive is based on their observation skills, using all five senses. This is a perishable skill that must be practiced.

  • Describing People: Be systematic (head-to-toe). Note race, sex, age, and any unique features (scars, tattoos, manner of walking/speaking).
  • Describing Places: Be systematic (left-to-right or top-to-bottom). Paint a picture with words.
  • Describing Things: Look for distinguishing marks, color, size, shape, texture, location, and type. Note damage or after-market enhancements on vehicles.

Report Structure & Content

Chronological: Arrangement of information in order of occurrence. Easiest to write and understand.

Categorical: Arrangement of information by category (e.g., witnesses, suspects, evidence).

  • Identification of the involved persons, vehicles, etc.
  • Narrative description of the offense or incident.
  • Elements of the offense or probable cause.

1. Synopsis: A brief summary of the case. Should include the when/where/how of police involvement, what crime occurred, and the case disposition.

2. Narrative: The detailed story of what happened, told in chronological order. Set the stage and stick to the main storyline.

3. Supplemental: For extra details that would distract from the narrative, such as evidence packaging, procedural matters, or statements proving culpability.

A victim of a sexual offense, stalking, trafficking, family violence, or child abduction may choose a pseudonym to designate their identity in all public files. Agency policy dictates how this is documented. Releasing the info is a Class C Misdemeanor.

Report Models & Scenarios

Report Writing Models

1. Detective Model

  • Written as you experienced the call.
  • Works well for simple cases.
  • Can be hard to follow in cases with multiple, coherent witness accounts.

2. Time-Line Model

  • Events are organized on a time-line first.
  • Report is written chronologically from that time-line.
  • Clearer in cases with conflicting or multiple witness accounts.

Practice Scenario 1: Domestic Violence

At 1630 you are dispatched to a domestic disturbance at 2315 Georgetown Drive. At 1636, you make contact.

"...Dad was really mad and started screaming at me... Mom stepped in between us. Dad pushed her against the wall... Mom's head hit the wall and the picture fell. Dad then grabbed her arm and threw her in the chair and knocked over the yellow lamp. Mom told me to run... Dad slapped her in the face with his left hand..."

Practice Scenario 2: Homicide

At 1006hrs, you are dispatched to 2217 Montague Rd (Carriage House Apts) for a female screaming near the mailboxes. As you arrive, a second caller from the Shell gas station across the street reports seeing a man punching a woman in the grassy area near the road.

You arrive and find a deceased white female with multiple stab wounds in the grassy area. While attending to her, dispatch advises the second caller saw the suspect run across the street between the house at 2220 Montague Rd and the trees.

Your Task: How would you write the synopsis for this report? What key details must be in the opening of your narrative?
